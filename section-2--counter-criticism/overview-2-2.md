# simpler headers

1. kill gameplay is a position. when i say "kill gameplay" and assert that challenges are evil, math is fake, and democracy is bad, all these subsequent claims are positions themselves, or representative of some position. this is true of all such positional statements, whoever presents it. people embody different values to greater and lesser degrees over the course of their lives.

2. gameplay is evil, gameplay is fake, and gameplay is bad. what does it mean to kill gameplay? rather than draw an arbitrary and undefinable line between good and bad compulsion (addiction and gambling vs. fun and rewarding challenges), i sought to create a distinctly defined but likely impossible to achieve ideal, something real to shoot for and fail at. in general, i find the definition of gameplay as 'anything that the player does in a game', and similarly the definition of game design as 'making any decisions that alter the game or player experience', to be completely useless. **gameplay**, as i kill it, is value systems divorced from their underlying values, perhaps replaced with different values, for any purpose; think more 'kill gamification' than 'kill gamefeel'. if a person says they play sudoku because it "makes them feel smart," i am compelled to point out that feeling smart is not the same as being smart; this is gameplay. if a person says they play a game because it makes them feel they are doing something meaningful or accomplishing something when they are really just increasing an integer which has been bound to the idea of progress, this is gameplay.

3. fun is a mood; gameplay is not a mood. engaging in gameplay can be fun, but the presence of fun is irrelevant. that is not to say fun is valueless, but it has no bearing on whether a person is engaging in "value systems divorced from their underlying values." kill gameplay is a position. positions are limited.

4. in past explorations of kill gameplay i advocated for 'blood', emotional vulnerability, expressive value. my views on this, personally, have evolved somewhat. they were an attempt to determine the core of kill gameplay, the 'answer' to what fills the void created. however, kill gameplay is a formal complaint, and i find blood to be as irrelevant as fun.

5. a defense employed against kill gameplay is a position that gameplay, in the 'anything that the player does in a game' sense, is sacred and important. i agree, of course. to "kill gameplay" from the perspective of a person who makes games or anything really is not to say that one must make something that literally cannot be interacted with. rather, kill gameplay represents the pursuit of an impossible ideal: 'anything that the player does in a game' is so sacred and so important that it must belong to them completely. no design that intentionally tampers with this is acceptable. no conception of games-as-art that includes the player's action as part of the artwork is tolerable. kill "gameplay" as in the word, the tendency to imagine binding a player's play to the game's game, to imprison free movement within a more rigid structure. kill gameplay, free movement.

6. what does it look like to make a game without gameplay? again, it is an impossible ideal, but it is a game-making process that does not concern itself with the player's actions, that understands the player's free movement is vitally important to life but has nothing to do with the meaning it must embody and convey, and that produces an artifact that can be navigated regardless.

7. gameplay makes a good enemy because it is infinite.

8. kill gameplay is gameplay, but don't tell me that.

9. gameplay is infinite. a game is finite.

10. kill gameplay.
